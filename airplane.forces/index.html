Aerodynamics & Phugoid Modeling Guide

What are we modeling?

Unlike a heavy commercial jet, a paper airplane operates in a regime of very low speeds, high drag, and is a **pure glider** once thrown. This simulation features a complete 2D translation and rotation engine.

Phugoid Oscillations (Glide Cycles)

Ever wondered why a paper airplane climbs, stalls, dips, and sweeps up again repeatedly? That is the classic **Phugoid Mode**:

  1. When thrown fast, the excess speed creates high **Lift ($L > F_g$)**, causing the plane to climb.
  2. As it climbs, kinetic energy transforms into potential energy (altitude). It slows down.
  3. As the velocity drops, the lift collapses. The nose dips under the force of gravity.
  4. As it dives, gravity accelerates the aircraft, building up speed and starting the loop all over again.

Key Parameter Relationships

  • Wing Surface Area ($S$): Increasing this will produce dramatic lift, enabling slow glides, but increases overall drag.
  • Drag Coefficient ($C_{D0}$): A "Dart" profile has an ultra-low $C_{D0}$ (~0.04), allowing high speed pierce, whereas wide-winged aircraft struggle with higher air friction (~0.15).
  • Atmospheric Wind: Headwinds increase the *relative air velocity*, meaning throwing your plane directly into the wind can produce highly dramatic initial climbs.
